# 5 Mistakes Brands Make When Working With Content Creators

**slug:** mistakes-brands-make-with-creators
**date:** 2026-03-25
**category:** Guide
**excerpt:** Most brand-creator collaborations underperform not because of bad content, but because of bad process. Here are the 5 most common mistakes I see — and how to fix them.
**readTime:** 6 min

---

After 8+ years and 190+ branded posts, I've seen every type of collaboration. The ones that work beautifully and the ones that crash and burn. Almost always, the difference isn't the creator or even the product — it's the process.

Here are the 5 mistakes I see brands make most often, and what to do instead.

## Mistake #1: Treating creators like billboards

The most common mistake, and the most damaging.

**What it looks like:**
"Post this photo with this caption at this time with these hashtags."

**Why it fails:**
You're not hiring a billboard. You're hiring a person whose audience trusts them specifically because they're authentic. The moment you strip away that authenticity, you're paying premium creator rates for content that performs like a generic ad.

**What to do instead:**
Share your goals and key messages. Let the creator translate them into content that fits their voice. You'll get content that actually engages their audience, not content that looks like it was assembled by a marketing committee.

## Mistake #2: Unclear or shifting scope

This happens more than you'd think, and it kills the relationship fast.

**The pattern:**
- Brief says: 1 Reel
- After filming: "Could you also do 2 Stories?"
- After editing: "Actually, can we use this in our ads too?"
- After posting: "Can you keep it up for 12 months instead of 3?"

Each of these is additional work. When the scope expands without the budget expanding, the creator feels taken advantage of. Even if they agree to it once, they won't work with you again.

**What to do instead:**
Define everything upfront in the contract:
- Number and type of deliverables
- Usage rights (organic only? paid ads? duration?)
- Number of revision rounds
- Timeline and deadlines

If the scope changes mid-project (it happens), acknowledge it and adjust the compensation. A simple "We'd love to add Stories — what would that cost?" goes a long way.

## Mistake #3: Too many approvals, too many cooks

I've had collaborations where the content was reviewed by:
1. The marketing manager
2. The brand manager
3. The agency account manager
4. The legal team
5. The CEO's assistant

By the time everyone gave their (contradictory) feedback, the trending audio I'd used was dead, the seasonal moment had passed, and the content felt like it was designed by committee — because it was.

**What to do instead:**
- Designate ONE person as the point of contact
- Internal alignment should happen BEFORE the brief goes to the creator
- Limit revisions to 1-2 rounds maximum
- Trust that the creator knows their platform better than your legal team does

The Hellmann's campaign that got 2.2M views? One brief, one round of feedback, done. Total time from brief to published: 10 days.

## Mistake #4: Ignoring the creator's audience data

Every experienced creator knows their audience intimately — demographics, peak posting times, which formats perform best, what topics resonate.

**What brands do wrong:**
- Demand posting times that don't match when the creator's audience is online
- Insist on formats (like carousels) when the creator's Reels consistently outperform
- Push messaging that doesn't resonate with the creator's specific demographic
- Ignore the creator's suggestions because "our marketing strategy says otherwise"

**What to do instead:**
Ask the creator: "What works best for your audience?" Then listen. They've spent years building that relationship. They know things about their followers that no analytics dashboard can tell you.

When a creator tells me "my audience responds best to morning posts with recipe content," and a brand insists on an evening fashion post — the brand is essentially paying to ignore the best available data.

## Mistake #5: Measuring the wrong things

This is the most subtle mistake, but it might be the most costly.

**What brands often measure:**
- Likes (vanity metric that doesn't correlate with purchasing decisions)
- Follower count of the creator (bigger isn't always better)
- Number of comments (easily inflated, hard to action)

**What they should measure:**
- **Saves** — the strongest signal that someone wants to come back to the content
- **Shares** — organic amplification beyond the creator's audience
- **Story replies and DMs** — genuine engagement and purchase intent
- **Link clicks / swipe-ups** — direct action metrics
- **Cost per quality engagement** — not cost per impression

A post with 500 likes but 200 saves is worth more than a post with 5,000 likes and 10 saves. The first one created genuine interest. The second one was just pretty.

I always provide performance reports to my brand partners with metrics that actually matter. If your creator doesn't offer this, ask for it.

## The meta-mistake: treating it as a transaction

All five mistakes above stem from the same root cause: treating creator partnerships as media buys instead of creative collaborations.

When you buy a billboard, you control every pixel and measure impressions. When you partner with a creator, you're tapping into a trusted relationship between a person and their audience. The rules are different.

**The brands that get the best ROI from me:**
- Brief quickly, approve quickly
- Trust my creative judgment
- Measure what matters
- Pay fairly and on time
- Think in terms of months, not posts

It's not complicated. But it requires a mindset shift from "we're buying ad space" to "we're partnering with a creative professional."
